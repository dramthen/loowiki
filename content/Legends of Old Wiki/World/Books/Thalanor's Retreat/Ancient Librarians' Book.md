I. On the Founding of the City
Thalanos was born not from
conquest, but necessity. The
cliffs above the Azure Sea held
little promise, yet beneath them
lay a cove calm enough to birth
harbors, and caves deep enough
to birth ambition. The first
stones were laid by wind-burnt
fishermen and wave-butten
saltwives, who carved channels
through coral and call them
streets.

But it was not long before steel
followed salt. The Pact--young
then, still radiant in purpose--
saw in Thalanos a gate. A place
to watch the water, and guard
what might come from it. They
raised bastions. Taught law.
Took tithes.

The harbor became a fortress.
The fortress became a capital.

II. The Reach of Empire

From the sea came trade. From
the trade came gold. With gold,
the Thalanosian governors
reached east into the lowlands,
paving the cracked spines of old
roads with banners and stone.

Slate's Pass was the first to fall
under the city's sphere. A
mountain corridor, too narrow
for armies but perfect for
tribute. Caravaners passed
through it in chains and with
coin, both paying the toll.

Kaelun's Crossing came next, at
spearpoint and scripture. Where
the western peaks of Mount
Craen sheared down into the
basilisk-ridden lowlands and
broke upon the idle ocean, it
bowed beneath the boat. The
Pact's paladins came bearing
flame and verse--burning
votives in the cliffside temples
and raising shrines to the One
Flame in their place.

Beyond it, the Dreadwood
loomed. The empire never fully
tamed that place. The trees
there whisper names that no
census recorded. Still,
watchtowers were built along its
edge, manned by conscripts too
green or damned for coastal
posts.

Dhorim's Hollow was stranger.
A sunken hole in the ground,,
thick with mists and the remains
of older things--structures with
no mortar, symbols with no
priesthood. Thalanos built no
walls there. Only outposts. They
came and went, often without
word. Faint laughter could be
heard through the caverns,
engendering tales of deep,
mining dwarves ready to snatch
children from their beds.

At last, the empire looked west
again--out to sea. It named itself
steward of the waves, cast light
from its lighthouses across
waters no map completed. The
Empire spoke of vigilance. The
sailors spoke of what watched
back.

III. The Reaver Question
They were called Reavers--first
in whispers, later in decrees. Not
a tribe, not a faith, nor a school
of magic, but something harder
to root: a hunter. They did not
march beneath banners of seize
thrones. They simply spread--
among dockworkers with
blackened veins, nobles who
woke screaming, children who
could not stomach bread but
craved bone.

in the early days, the city treated
them as a symptom. A cult, a
curse, a vice. Paladins were sent
from the High Pact, not in open
war but in correction. A quiet
purse. Arrets, burnings,
midnight trials that were never
written into the court ledgers.

Yet something about the
Reavers defied the usual
response. They didn't resist--
not openly--but each time the
city pressed, the sickness moved
deeper. Into the cisterns. The
catacombs. The faith.

Elandra of the Hall arrived then,
with her Shieldbearer--one of
the last recorded bindings of
soul to sword. They said her
light could make a liar confess
just by stepping into it. She
called for caution. She said the
city trembled beneath a deeper
wound.

She was right.

Before the Reavers could be
undone, before the city could
even name what it faced, the sky
cracked.

Not a bolt, nor a tremor, but the
end of coherence. Streetlamps
lit in the day. The tide came in
twice and never went out. The
Flame-chapels reported prayers
returning out of order--
blessings delivered before they
were spoken.

And then, as if someone had
misread the story of the world:
Fracture.

You may wonder who I am to
write such words.

I wonder why you had to kill me
just to read my book.
